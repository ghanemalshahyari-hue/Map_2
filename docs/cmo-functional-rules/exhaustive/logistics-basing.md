# CMO Functional Rules — Logistics & Basing (exhaustive)

**Scope.** Everything *Command: Modern Operations* does for **logistics and basing**: how units
are hosted at airfields / ports / ships (the parasite-host model), how aircraft are armed and
turned around (ready/arm cycle, quick-turnaround, sortie accounting, flight-size), how bases store
ordnance (magazines, the unlimited-magazines flag, weapon-record vs magazine linkage), how airfields
and ports are physically composed and damaged (per-component HP/armor, access points, runway repair),
how cargo is modeled and moved (carrying-capacity budgets, containers, manual load/unload, cargo
missions, transfer vs delivery), how forces are resupplied at sea (Underway Replenishment / UNREP),
how aircraft are refueled in the air (buddy refueling, refuel waypoints), and how fuel/range is
predicted and managed (fuel estimates, range rings, the speed-vs-fuel curve). It also captures the
guided-weapon (PGM) families and their delivery constraints, since those gate what a base's stored
ordnance can actually do.

**This is the exhaustive pass for this bucket** — it is auto-generated by deduplicating and
organizing *every* rule extracted from *every* tutorial transcript classified into "Logistics &
Basing." Near-identical mechanics from different videos have been merged into a single rule that
cites all contributing sources.

**Caveat.** Source transcripts are YouTube **auto-generated captions**, so wording, unit names, and
numbers can contain transcription errors (some are flagged inline, e.g. "mis-stated"). **Stated
numbers are captured verbatim** and are almost always *per-database-unit illustrations from a
specific build*, **not universal constants**. Several behaviors are explicitly **build-version
dependent** (e.g. fuel dumps and control towers being non-functional in build 1.15.8.1). Rules marked
Low/Med confidence are inferred, from secondary mentions, or where the narrator was tentative.

## Hosting & basing (parasite-host model)

### Parasite / host-unit relationship (aircraft, ships, ports)
- **Models:** Units physically hosted aboard/at another unit (airbase, port, ship) until launched.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Host type (airfield / port / ship); hosted unit type (aircraft or ship); host capacity; per-aircraft location node within an airfield.
- **Behavior / rules:** A "parasite" unit shares another base/vessel. Two basic types: (1) **aircraft-type hosts** — anything that can carry aircraft shows a small icon in the bottom-right corner; (2) **ship-type hosts** — a port shows an icon meaning ships are present, and certain SHIPS can themselves carry other ships ("ship inception"). Access menus differ: ships via **Boat Operations (F7)** or the "boats" button; aircraft via **Aircraft Operations (F6)** or the aircraft button. An airfield is **NOT a single unit** — it is composed of many sub-pieces (runway, taxiway, access points, tarmac/parking, revetments, hangars, ammo/fuel dumps); selecting it marks where each individual aircraft sits, and ordering an aircraft to move shows it traverse those nodes to the runway. A host can be a combined port+airport.
- **Outputs / effects:** Hosted-unit lists, per-base aircraft placement markers, launch/boat menus.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Selecting two different airports at once produces a confusing merged list — avoid. In the scenario/mission editor an extra "edit hosted aircraft" button appears (lets you place a specific aircraft type at a specific node); it is absent outside the editor.
- **Source:** f_l2xgYKo8Y
- **Confidence:** High

### Ship launch from port (deployment delay, grouping, abort, auto-rearm)
- **Models:** Time and process for a docked ship to clear port and put to sea.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Selected ship(s); single vs shift-click multi-select; launch-as-group vs launch-individually; abort command; doctrine (withdraw & redeploy).
- **Behavior / rules:** Select a unit in Boat Operations and click **Launch** — the ship begins deploying out to sea. There is **~15 minutes** between issuing "go" and the ship actually clearing port. Shift-click multiple units to launch them as a group OR launch each individually (a way to pre-group ships into desired formations). An **Abort** button stops a launch in progress and keeps them in port. A docked ship is constantly reloaded, and **any ship at a dock is automatically refueled and rearmed** (modifiable via the **Doctrine > Withdraw and Redeploy** page).
- **Outputs / effects:** Ship transitions from docked to underway; group assignment; rearm/refuel state.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Ships in port take damage if the port is attacked (e.g., enemy bombers). Doctrine can be dialed per launched ship to e.g. not launch SAMs.
- **Source:** f_l2xgYKo8Y
- **Confidence:** High

### Self-built basing facilities (helipad / barracks / ammo pad / AV-gas)
- **Models:** Operators can construct a base from component facilities (helipads, barracks, ammo pad, aviation-gas storage) that then support sortie/cargo/rearm operations.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Component facilities placed by the editor: helipads, barracks (also acts as a cargo holder), ammo pad, AV-gas storage.
- **Behavior / rules:** Qualitative: a self-built helport is assembled from helipads + a barracks + an ammo pad + AV gas; the AV gas matters because the facility "becomes a target" (fuel storage is a vulnerability). The **barracks doubles as a cargo holder** for loading infantry. No numeric capacities stated.
- **Outputs / effects:** A functioning helo base capable of launching transports and holding cargo/ammo/fuel.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Fuel/AV-gas storage is a destructible vulnerability if the base is attacked; no stated capacity numbers.
- **Source:** yqZ7VHn6EFI

## Airfield & port structure, damage, and repair

### Airfield component HP/armor model (what each structure does)
- **Models:** An airfield is a set of damageable sub-objects with individual HP/armor that gate different functions.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Per-component HP and armor; component type (runway, taxiway, access point, tarmac/revetment, hangar, ammo dump, fuel dump, control tower).
- **Behavior / rules:** Stated values (build **1.15.8.1**): **Runway** 3200 m long, 1200 damage points, **HEAVY armor** (only penetrators/very-high-capability weapons can damage it). **Taxiway** 1200 hit points, LESS armor than the runway, still usable as a runway. **Access point** 1000 hit points, **NO armor** (3 GBUs / three Mk-84s suffice). **Hangar** ~600 damage points (mis-stated 602), NO armor — easy to kill, and one hit destroys whatever is inside; you **cannot see hangar contents on recon** unless you observe an aircraft leaving. **Underground/protected aircraft shelters:** special armor, 1600 damage points. **Ammo bunkers:** very high armor + high HP — effectively unkillable (≈12 GBUs claimed, said "50" hyperbolically) unless they're mere revetments. **Fuel dumps** ~40 hit points, light armor (a cannon could kill them). **Control tower** exists but is essentially cosmetic in this build.
- **Outputs / effects:** Component destruction toggles base capabilities (see related rules in this section).
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Hangar contents hidden from recon; tarmac/revetments can be "seen into" to ID aircraft type if recon is good.
- **Source:** -z_O0kvH5zI
- **Confidence:** High

### Runway/taxiway damage, repair, and access-point disabling
- **Models:** Surfaces self-repair over time; the base goes offline only if all access points are down.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Damage dealt to surface; nuclear vs conventional hit; number of access points destroyed (**4 total**); repair timer.
- **Behavior / rules:** A "destroyed" runway is **not truly destroyed unless hit by a NUCLEAR weapon** — otherwise it self-repairs over time depending on damage taken, with a repair time **on the order of 24 hours** (not 20 minutes). To keep a runway down you must re-strike it continually. CMO simulates a small connector requiring an **ACCESS POINT** to reach either the runway or taxiway; there are **4 access points**. Destroying all four puts the airport **out of commission for about a day**. If you miss even **ONE** access point the airport remains fully functional (can still launch). Aircraft physically traverse the base when launching: they exit hangars, occupy access points, then occupy the runway before takeoff (and the markers disappear in that order) — meaning spying can ID aircraft as they move.
- **Outputs / effects:** Temporary base shutdown (~1 day) only with all 4 access points down; surfaces regenerate over ~24h.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Only a nuke makes a runway permanently destroyed; partial access-point kills accomplish nothing; aircraft-on-base movement is observable by recon.
- **Source:** -z_O0kvH5zI
- **Confidence:** High

### Ammo bunkers gate rearming (when ammo is not unlimited)
- **Models:** Destroying the magazine bunkers removes a base's ability to rearm with the stored weapons.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Scenario unlimited-ammo flag (off); magazine/weapons set up by the designer in the bunker; bunker HP/armor.
- **Behavior / rules:** When a scenario does **NOT** use unlimited ammunition, designers set up a magazine at the airport holding the intended weapons; everything inside those bunkers is vulnerable. If the bunker is destroyed AND all its ammo killed, the airport **can no longer rearm with those weapons** (it can still **refuel**). Aircraft that already have those weapons **loaded** are unaffected. Practically the bunkers are so heavily armored/high-HP they are **not worth targeting** — guidance is to ignore them and win via access points instead.
- **Outputs / effects:** Loss of rearm capability for the stored weapon types; refuel unaffected.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Correctly modeled only when ammo is finite; under "unlimited magazines" bunkers are irrelevant.
- **Source:** -z_O0kvH5zI
- **Confidence:** High

### Fuel dumps and control tower are non-functional (this build)
- **Models:** Some real-world-critical airfield structures have no simulated effect.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Fuel-dump destruction; control-tower destruction; build version **1.15.8.1**.
- **Behavior / rules:** Destroying **every fuel dump** at a base has **NO effect** — the base can still refuel and operate aircraft for the rest of the game (fuel dumps "don't matter" in this build, stated three times). Likewise destroying the **control tower** makes essentially no difference to airport operations in this build (kept as a "feel-good" last target).
- **Outputs / effects:** No change to refuel/launch capability when fuel dumps or tower destroyed.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Explicitly **version-dependent** ("this can change as time goes on"); real-world these would be decisive.
- **Source:** -z_O0kvH5zI
- **Confidence:** High

### Airfield strike arithmetic and per-target damage distribution (WRA)
- **Models:** Hits-to-kill = target HP / per-bomb damage; AI bunching defeated by per-target attack limits.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Target HP; per-bomb damage value; aircraft count & weapons-per-aircraft; WRA "units attacking per target" = 1; weapon-impacts-per-target setting; flight-size & "force flight size".
- **Behavior / rules:** Hits needed = **ceil(target HP / per-bomb damage)**. Worked examples: access point 1000 HP, 500 kg bomb does **220** damage → 1000/220 = needs **5** direct hits; 4 aircraft × ~4 bombs ≈ 20 bombs covers it. PGM example: access point 1000 HP, GBU-24/LGB ~**250–300** damage → **4** successful hits each (round up to 12 bombs for two targets). Hangar example: GBU-10 does **643** damage > ~600 HP hangar → **ONE** bomb per hangar kills it. CONVENTIONAL (unguided) strikes: do **NOT** put all four access points in one mission — aircraft drop on one, see it survive, and RTB with the others untouched (only one access point properly damaged). Selecting the whole airport + "add all units" only attacks units nearest your launch axis (useless). Separate each access point into its **OWN mission** (sometimes two works). To spread damage across multiple targets in one mission, set WRA to only **ONE** unit attacking per target and **weapon-impacts-per-target = 1**, so each bomber hits its own target; without this, the whole posse dumps everything on the top targets and lower ones survive. Larger flights spread damage wider; smaller flights re-attack faster.
- **Outputs / effects:** Distributed independently-targeted bomb stacks; predictable kills when math/WRA are correct.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Auto-generated flights re-attack the same target endlessly (must manually order re-attacks or split missions); ballistic-computer aircraft are inaccurate so over-provision; "force flight size" must be disabled when intentionally over-sizing a flight.
- **Source:** -z_O0kvH5zI
- **Confidence:** High

### AI auto-target selection on a strike mission picks "best" aimpoints (often ammo bunkers)
- **Models:** When you order a strike/direct mission against a base, the engine auto-selects which sub-targets (bunkers, runway, tarmac) to hit, which may not match operator intent.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Mission type (land strike / direct mission), target base composition (ammo bunkers, runway, tarmac, hangars), weapon-release authorization, number of weapons authorized ("use all weapons against target").
- **Behavior / rules:** Ordering a **direct mission** on a base lets bombers gravitate to **ammo bunkers** as targets rather than the operator's preferred center-of-base aimpoint. To control the aimpoint precisely you instead **manually target a specific point** (e.g. center of the airport) and set weapon-release authorization. Adding a unit to the mission can auto-create multiple sortie targets (it "tried to do four of these things" when only one was wanted) — control via not-auto-adding the unit and configuring single vs. multiple targets. **"Use all weapons against target"** dumps the full rack on the aimpoint.
- **Outputs / effects:** Set of struck aimpoints; with manual targeting + "use all weapons" the chosen point receives the entire weapon load.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Auto-target may prefer suboptimal aimpoints (ammo bunkers) vs. operator intent; adding the unit to a mission can spawn extra unwanted sorties; high time-acceleration can cause a bomber to fail to drop and need re-tasking.
- **Source:** Si71JPkREWk
- **Confidence:** Med

### Terrain / built-up land absorbs blast energy (damage vs. land type)
- **Models:** The land/terrain class under an impact point governs how much explosive energy reaches and destroys targets — urban/built-up terrain soaks energy and shields nearby targets.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Terrain class at the aimpoint (crop/shrubland vs. "built-up land" with a height value, e.g. 30 ft, classified urban), weapon yield, burst type (air burst vs. surface/ground impact).
- **Behavior / rules:** Hovering over map cells reveals terrain class; some areas flip between "crop lands/shrublands" and "built-up land 30 ft (urban)" over short distances. Dropping a large weapon onto built-up/urban terrain causes that terrain to "**suck all the energy out**" of the blast — even **13 megatons** on built-up terrain left many aircraft intact. Aiming the same weapon onto open/center terrain (avoiding the built-up elevation) lets the full effect propagate and annihilates everything. Therefore **aimpoint selection relative to terrain class matters more than raw yield**. Air burst vs. surface burst changes the damage "personality" — an air-burst weapon distributes damage differently than a ground impact.
- **Outputs / effects:** Effective lethal radius / target destruction varies dramatically with terrain class and burst mode at the aimpoint.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Built-up/urban terrain dramatically attenuates blast (counterintuitively huge yields underperform there); narrow terrain-class boundaries near a target mean small aimpoint shifts change results; air burst vs. impact yields different damage footprints; numbers (megatons) are stated qualitatively, no formula given.
- **Source:** Si71JPkREWk
- **Confidence:** Med

### Losses-and-expenditures / damage message log with per-weapon impact detail
- **Models:** The engine records exactly what each weapon destroyed and where it impacted, viewable per side and per weapon hit.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Weapon impacts, target inventory (aircraft, hangars, tarmac, command center), losses-and-expenditures view, message-log entries per munition.
- **Behavior / rules:** A **losses/expenditures** screen enumerates everything destroyed (every airplane, hangar, tarmac, etc.) and the munition expenditure (e.g. it "only cost four megatons"). The **damage message log** can be scrolled, and clicking a specific weapon entry (e.g. "B43") shows where it hit. The log distinguishes whether the weapon **impacted (ground burst)** vs. **air burst**, which corresponds to different damage. Some structures can survive while everything around them is destroyed (one hangar "was spared").
- **Outputs / effects:** Auditable per-target destruction record and per-weapon impact location/burst-type.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Random survivors possible (a hangar survived amid total destruction); burst-type recorded affects damage interpretation.
- **Source:** Si71JPkREWk
- **Confidence:** Med

## Magazines & ordnance stock

### Unlimited Magazines scenario toggle (and why to disable it)
- **Models:** A scenario-wide setting that gives every magazine an infinite, fully-stocked inventory.
- **Inputs / parameters:** **Editor > Scenario Features & Settings → "Unlimited Magazines at air/naval bases"** checkbox; the scenario-level unlimited-ammo flag generally.
- **Behavior / rules:** By default a freshly built airfield magazine is **empty**, so ready-arm offers nothing. When the flag is checked, **every loadout is available** and "number available" shows **unlimited** (e.g., nuclear bombs for everyone) — "I've got everything I could possibly want." With the flag OFF, magazines must be stocked explicitly per-weapon. The setting is the gate that makes the whole munitions-logistics workflow relevant: with unlimited ammo OFF, a depleted launcher (e.g. an SSGN that fired its Tomahawk rack) must obtain replacement munitions from somewhere physical — ideally a "**factory**", otherwise via the cargo/transfer logistics chain. **Designer warning:** unlimited magazines lets players spam JDAMs / expose powerful unintended weapons (a B61 nuke) and "ruin the fun," so designers often turn it OFF and stock magazines by hand.
- **Outputs / effects:** All weapons selectable with infinite stock (on); empty magazines requiring manual stocking, and a meaningful resupply economy (off).
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Turning it off reverts to empty magazines that must be filled per-weapon; "factory" is named as the ideal/native source of munitions.
- **Source:** f_l2xgYKo8Y; b3L5YTsUZLk; QiaqKwE6Fw8; Mj7PZ9nHYqI
- **Confidence:** High

### Air-base magazine ammunition limits gating loadouts
- **Models:** Finite stored ordnance at a base constrains which/how many loadouts can be armed.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Base magazine contents (per weapon); requested loadout; per-loadout weapon counts; "unlimited magazines" scenario flag.
- **Behavior / rules:** Each selectable loadout shows **how many instances** of that loadout the base can currently support given its magazines. Loadouts shown in **ITALICS** mean the base magazine **lacks enough ammunition** to support that loadout. A column lists how many loadouts of each style the base can support right now. Example: magazines hold 40 groups of four Sidewinders (mis-stated as 50) = ~200 Sidewinders at the base; the per-style count reflects this. A **top button filters to show ONLY currently usable loadouts**. If you arm a group larger than ammo allows, CMO loads the ones it can and **warns** "you've exceeded your limit" / "didn't have enough ammunition for all of them."
- **Outputs / effects:** Per-loadout availability counts; partial arming when stock insufficient.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Governed entirely by the unlimited-magazines flag (see that rule). Under unlimited magazines every loadout shows unlimited.
- **Source:** f_l2xgYKo8Y
- **Confidence:** High

### Stock magazines via "Add Loadout to Magazine" shortcut (vs manual add-weapons)
- **Models:** A fast way to populate a base magazine's weapon TYPES by harvesting them from aircraft loadouts.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Aircraft ready-armed with the desired loadouts (in scenario editor, "ready immediately"); right-click → "Add loadout to magazine"; an existing magazine to receive types.
- **Behavior / rules:** **Manual method:** open Magazines → **Add Weapons**, optionally tick "**show only weapons compatible with aircraft hosted by [base]**" to filter, then add each weapon — tedious. **Shortcut:** ready-arm several aircraft with all the loadouts you want available, then right-click each and "**Add loadout to magazine**" — this populates the magazine with those weapon **TYPES**. Roughly idempotent: re-adding when "already have everything" is a no-op; presenter re-adds repeatedly to be safe after missing clicks.
- **Outputs / effects:** Magazine populated with the union of weapon types from the chosen loadouts (quantities set separately).
- **Edge cases / quirks:** The "compatible with aircraft hosted by [base]" filter trims the manual list. Adding loadout sets the TYPES present, not final quantities.
- **Source:** b3L5YTsUZLk
- **Confidence:** High

### Setting magazine quantities; Reserve-Available ejects mounted weapons back into the magazine
- **Models:** After types are present, you dial counts per weapon, and you can recover weapons currently on aircraft into stock.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Per-weapon quantity fields in Munitions (type a number; clear with 0 + backspace; supports Ctrl+A/Ctrl+V); aircraft set to "Reserve available" via ready-arm.
- **Behavior / rules:** In the magazine's **Munitions** list you set quantities per weapon (examples: 100 drop tanks, 200 JASSM, 1000 of one item). After stocking, ordering the loaded aircraft to "**Reserve available**" (ready-arm) **EJECTS everything on their wings into the magazine**, increasing stock. The magazines then show all wanted possibilities ready to use.
- **Outputs / effects:** Defined per-weapon quantities; mounted weapons returned to magazine on reserve-available.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Quantity edit quirks: 0+backspace to clear, Ctrl+A/Ctrl+V to bulk-set. Reserve-available is also how you "dump" a just-loaded loadout back if you ready-armed by mistake.
- **Source:** b3L5YTsUZLk
- **Confidence:** High

### Ready-Arm consumes from the magazine; only stocked weapons are selectable; arming takes time
- **Models:** Live re-arming draws down finite magazine stock and is restricted to what the magazine holds.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Magazine contents and quantities; selected loadout/weapons at ready-arm; arming time.
- **Behavior / rules:** With limited (non-unlimited) magazines, ready-arm shows **ONLY the weapons present** in the magazine as selectable. Choosing weapons and arming **DECREASES the magazine quantities** accordingly ("my magazines have decreased in quantities, exactly what we wanted"). "**Ready**" (not "Ready immediately", which is editor-only) starts a re-arm process that **takes time**. "**Ready immediately**" is a scenario-editor convenience that arms instantly; in live play arming consumes time.
- **Outputs / effects:** Aircraft armed with chosen weapons; magazine stock decremented; arming timer in live play.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Accidentally hitting "Ready immediately" (editor) vs "Ready." Excess weapons removed during a re-arm are dumped back into the magazine.
- **Source:** b3L5YTsUZLk
- **Confidence:** High

### Aircraft carriers come with PRELOADED magazines; all base/carrier magazine mechanics are identical
- **Models:** Carriers behave like airbases for magazines but ship pre-stocked.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Carrier unit (e.g. Nimitz class) and its magazines.
- **Behavior / rules:** Unlike a freshly built airfield (empty magazine), a carrier "**comes preloaded and ready to go.**" Its magazines are ready to be filled further — you can add HARM/AARGM/etc. via the same Munitions list, and the add-loadout-to-magazine / quantity workflow works identically. Everything that works for an airbase magazine works the same on a carrier.
- **Outputs / effects:** Carrier with pre-stocked magazines, extensible via the same tools.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Be mindful that carriers start **pre-loaded** (don't assume empty). Same flow applies to any aircraft-hosting structure.
- **Source:** b3L5YTsUZLk
- **Confidence:** High

### Weapon-record vs. magazine linkage for reloads
- **Models:** A ground/launcher unit can only reload and fire a munition that is explicitly defined as one of its loaded **weapon records** (the firing mounts), not merely present in its **magazine** stores.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Per-platform weapon records, per-unit/parent magazine contents (ammo stores), reload-priority flag, scenario-editor mode (to edit weapon records freely).
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) Adding a new munition type to a unit's magazine **alone does NOT** let the unit fire it: the launcher must have an electronic "link" to that weapon record. (2) Setting "reload priority" on a magazine-only munition leaves the team "ready to fire" but unable to actually fire. (3) To make a munition usable you must **reduce/empty an existing weapon-record slot** (set its loaded count to 0) and then "**Change**" the launcher's weapon record to load 1 of the desired munition; only then does it show in **blue** and is "ready to fire." (4) After firing, the launcher auto-reloads from magazine; if only one munition type remains it reloads that (e.g. falls back to the Spirals when they are the last stock). (5) Setting "reload priority" on the chosen weapon record makes the system swap a fresh round of that type in after a shot. (6) Reload can intermittently fail ("cannot reload the weapon at this time") because of the same missing-link constraint. (7) Many of these edits only take effect while the game is **PAUSED**. The technique generalizes to other reloadable platforms (e.g. torpedoes).
- **Outputs / effects:** Launcher weapon record changes to the new munition (shown blue / ready-to-fire); magazine counts decrement; after-fire auto-reload populates the next round per reload-priority/available stock.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Magazine-only ammo yields a unit that reports "ready to fire" but never fires; reload via the magazine button often won't physically swap a fired missile for a new type; "no reloads available" until a weapon record exists; some changes silently ignored unless paused; building it from scratch in the editor (correct weapon records + magazine) avoids the whole problem.
- **Source:** 1A7ltgegnQI
- **Confidence:** Med

### Generic-unit / add-weapon-record fabrication of launchers (editor)
- **Models:** In the scenario editor you can synthesize an arbitrary launcher (e.g. an SSM silo) and bolt new weapon records onto any existing mount, with capacity auto-limited by the mount.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Generic unit type (searched by keyword, e.g. "SSM" / "generic rocket"), an existing weapon mount on the unit (e.g. a 7.62mm machine-gun mount), an "add weapon record" action, a requested round count.
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) Create a generic unit and type a class to instantiate it (battery, silo, trailer, etc.). (2) Select an existing mount and "**add weapon record**", typing in the desired munition. (3) The system **caps how many fit** (the example landed on ~80) — you request a count and it accepts up to the mount's capacity. (4) Must be **paused** for the change to apply. The fabricated unit then fires the added munition normally (shown firing a ballistic rocket from a converted MG mount).
- **Outputs / effects:** A new firing-capable unit/weapon record; the unit can immediately be tasked to fire the added munition.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Only available in scenario-editor mode; capacity is mount-bound (you can't load arbitrarily many); changes ignored if not paused.
- **Source:** 1A7ltgegnQI
- **Confidence:** Med

## Aircraft arming, turnaround & sortie generation

### Aircraft ready/arm cycle and ready-time (sustained vs surge tempo)
- **Models:** Time to service, arm, refuel, repair and maintain an aircraft before it can launch.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Aircraft type/generation/service; chosen loadout; mission air-operations tempo (sustained vs surge); magazine stock.
- **Behavior / rules:** Select aircraft, pick a loadout (left-click; shift-click to multi-select), then **Ready/Arm** (button or right-click). Two arm options: "**OK, Ready**" (loads everything shown) and "**OK, Ready (exclude optional weapons)**" — the latter only available if the loadout has weapons flagged optional; it skips optional weapons (saves ammo, **does NOT load faster**). Once readying, aircraft go **YELLOW**; when done they go **GREEN** (launchable). Stated ready time for a Sidewinder loadout = **3 hours**; this time also includes refilling, repairing and maintaining the aircraft. Under a "**Sustained Air Operations tempo**" arming is drastically slowed: an AH-1J TOW/FFAR loadout showed a time-to-ready of **20 hours**. In a normal mission the player usually does **NOT** control sustained vs surge — must plan around it; some scenarios give a surge in the morning then sustained afterward. Switching tempo does **not** retroactively change an aircraft already counting down (an F-100 kept its prior time-to-ready after a mode switch).
- **Outputs / effects:** Aircraft readiness state (yellow→green); time-to-ready clock; partial arming on low stock.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** In a normal (non-editor) mission, set-time-to-ready / rename / remove buttons are not available. Quick-turnaround sustained-vs-surge interacts with re-arm timing.
- **Source:** f_l2xgYKo8Y
- **Confidence:** High

### Quick-turnaround (rapid rearm/refuel) limits
- **Models:** Ground crews pre-staged to rapidly rearm/refuel a returning aircraft, with strict caps.
- **Inputs / parameters:** "Enable quick" toggle per aircraft; sortie counter; cumulative airborne-hours counter.
- **Behavior / rules:** "**Enable quick**" sets ground crews to quickly rearm and refuel that aircraft. **Hard limits** apply: a maximum of **TWO sorties** under quick turnaround, and a **4-hour total cap** — you cannot fly 3h59m, land, quick-reload and repeat; once the 4-hour limit is reached you're done. Each **TAKEOFF counts as one sortie** the instant the aircraft leaves the ground, whether or not it actually went anywhere.
- **Outputs / effects:** Quick-turnaround status; consumed sortie count; airborne-hours toward the 4h cap.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** A takeoff with no engagement still burns a sortie — be careful not to launch unnecessarily.
- **Source:** f_l2xgYKo8Y
- **Confidence:** High

### Sortie activation on takeoff (full rearm penalty)
- **Models:** Any airborne sortie forces the full ready/arm cycle on return, even if no ordnance was expended.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Takeoff event; aircraft loadout/ready time; fuel-state and loadout doctrine settings.

## Air-to-air refueling & range extension

### Buddy (air-to-air) refueling as range extension
- **Models:** A tanker transfers fuel to a whole receiving flight simultaneously, refilling to max then disconnecting.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Receiver flight & fuel state; provider (tanker) selection ("select provider"); tanker speed (must drop to LOITER while refueling); receiver max fuel allotment; mission A2A refueling option "tanker follows receiver's flight plan".
- **Behavior / rules:** Order receivers to refuel and "**select provider**" (the tanker). The **TANKER must stay at loiter speed** while refueling (it auto-slows when receivers approach); if commanded to military speed it cannot refuel. The whole flight (e.g., a 6-ship) takes on fuel **together** — the tanker acts as "the world's largest external fuel tank" filling all aircraft simultaneously. Receivers **disconnect once they've received their MAXIMUM allotment** of fuel, then follow/escort the tanker; you re-order refuel as needed. Doing this **manually in a relay** (refuel → escort → refuel again) extends range effectively **INDEFINITELY** as long as the tanker has fuel. In a mission, the A2A-refueling config has "**tanker follows receiver's flight plan**" which automates this, BUT the mission tanker **returns when done** — only the manual method continues indefinitely to a chosen destination.
- **Outputs / effects:** Receiver fuel topped to max; greatly extended effective range (demo: ~100–150 mi F-4s extended to ~1000 nm).
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Provider locked to loiter speed during transfer; the tanker's own low-fuel state is shown by a **blue circle**; mission-mode auto-refuel ends with tanker RTB whereas manual relay does not.

### Aircraft fuel / range gating and en-route refuel waypoints (basing-return logistics)
- **Models:** An aircraft has finite fuel/range; reaching/returning to base may require tanker refueling, and refueling can be forced via flight-plan waypoints or post-hoc refuel orders.

(2) You can **insert a waypoint** between existing waypoints and convert it to a "**refueling waypoint**" so the aircraft switches to refuel mode, takes on needed fuel, then continues. (3) Waypoints carry roles (**ingress / turning point / egress**) and the flight plan validates them (e.g. a weapon target must be tied to a waypoint; warnings appear if no targets). (5) You can right-click an aircraft, **unassign** it, and immediately "**order to refuel**" (optionally clicking the specific tanker) and it diverts for gas. ## Fuel, range & endurance management

### Waypoint fuel-required estimate (F2 on final waypoint)
- **Models:** Predicting total fuel a platform will burn to traverse a plotted course, so the operator can confirm it won't run out of gas en route.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Selected unit's plotted course/waypoints; the unit's current ordered throttle/speed setting at the moment of query; unit's onboard fuel quantity (e.g., FFG-36 Underwood = 586 tons); external stores/loadout (modifies aircraft consumption).
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) Plot a course (waypoints) for the selected unit. (2) Click the **final waypoint** and press **F2** to open an estimate panel. (3) The panel shows "**total fuel quantity required**" for the full course computed at the unit's **CURRENT** speed/throttle setting. (4) Operator compares required vs. onboard manually (e.g. 586 tons − 375034 [units] = 211 tons remaining in the demo) to judge whether the trip is safe. (5) Changing the throttle setting and re-querying F2 yields a different required figure (cruise/full/flank produce different totals). The estimate updates as the platform's actual speed spins up, so it is only accurate once the unit reaches the queried speed.
- **Outputs / effects:** Displays predicted total fuel required for the course at current speed; read-only estimate (no state mutation).
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Estimate is tied to the speed at the instant of the query — right after changing throttle the platform hasn't "spun up" so the number is not yet correct; let speed stabilize. Aircraft consumption shown is "modified by external stores," so the displayed per-minute burn can differ a lot from baseline — use the stores-adjusted number. You can change the speed order between queries to find the fastest speed that still completes the trip.
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** High

### Ship speed-vs-fuel cost curve (cubic / V³ drag relationship)
- **Models:** How a surface ship's fuel burn rises steeply (roughly with the cube of speed) as it goes faster, so small speed increases cost large amounts of fuel over long distances.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Ordered ship speed/throttle (cruise vs full ~30 kn vs flank); distance of the course; ship hydrodynamic drag model.
- **Behavior / rules:** Narrator states a ship uses "**V to the 3**" (V³) for the speed/energy required to go flank speed. Demonstrated on FFG-36 Underwood over a long transatlantic course: at **full** speed (~30 kn) fuel required = 475040 (vs 586,000 onboard — still makes it). At **flank** speed fuel required = 852 (852,000 — exceeds 586 onboard, trip impossible). At **31 kn** the requirement was ~608 (608,000); at **30 kn** it was ~474. So crossing from 30→31 kn changed the requirement by **~100 tons**; the narrator generalizes that **at those distances roughly "one knot of difference adds 100 tons of fuel."** Operator iteratively dials speed down until required ≤ onboard.
- **Outputs / effects:** Fuel-required estimate scales sharply (near-cubically) with ordered speed; determines whether a given speed can complete the trip.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** The "100 tons per 1 knot" figure is **distance-specific** (stated "at those kinds of distances"), not a universal constant — presented as illustrative of how sensitive ships are, NOT a fixed CMO formula. Flank speed can make a long trip outright impossible because cost exceeds capacity. The exact CMO drag exponent stated is V³ for flank-speed energy.
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** Med

### Aircraft dynamic range-ring projection
- **Models:** A visual map circle showing how far an aircraft can fly on remaining fuel at its current speed, updating live as throttle/speed changes.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Aircraft remaining fuel; current actual speed (not merely the ordered setting); throttle band (loiter / cruise / military / full ~470 kn for the Airbus example); external stores/loadout.
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) CMO draws a **blue range circle** around an aircraft on the map. (2) Selecting a throttle band changes the ring: **loiter = largest** ring, cruise = large, military = smaller, **full = smallest**. (3) The ring is recomputed from the aircraft's **INSTANTANEOUS** speed, so right after ordering a higher speed the ring does not immediately show the correct (smaller) range — it shrinks as the aircraft accelerates and drag increases toward peak speed (~470 kn). (4) You only get an accurate reading once the aircraft reaches the commanded steady speed. (5) Because range is locked to whatever speed you're actually holding, you can manually set a speed just below peak (e.g. 464 kn) to keep a ring that still reaches the destination, and it will hold that same range all the way there.
- **Outputs / effects:** Live map circle = reachable radius at current speed; shrinks/grows dynamically with speed and as the platform accelerates/decelerates.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Ring is **NOT accurate until cruise/commanded speed is reached**. Shrinkage during acceleration is due to rising drag, not the act of throttling up. Feature is **aircraft-only** in the demo — explicitly **absent on ships**. Tiny speed reductions buy large range near peak: dropping 464→462 kn added **~105 nautical miles** of range. Loiter gives a large INITIAL ring but the aircraft then decelerates and actual achievable range collapses (see cruise-optimum rule).
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** High

### Aircraft cruise-speed fuel optimum (penalty for off-cruise speeds)
- **Models:** Aircraft are most fuel-efficient at their designed cruise speed; flying slower (loiter/creep) or faster both waste fuel and reduce achievable range.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Throttle band (loiter/creep/cruise/military/full); aircraft cruise-speed design point; current speed.
- **Behavior / rules:** **Cruise throttle auto-targets the optimum** trade between speed and economy ("optimum ratio between speed and his ability to get there in a hurry... not burn too much fuel"). Switching an aircraft to **loiter** gives an immediate large range-ring boost, BUT as the aircraft slows it burns substantial fuel and net achievable range drops so much it can't reach the destination. Picking an intermediate speed between loiter and cruise has "the exact same impact" — still worse than cruise. Rule stated: "**Aircraft are designed for their cruise speeds. If you don't keep them at their cruise speeds, you're going to lose that optimum.**" Slightly trimming speed near cruise can marginally help (see peak-economy rule) but large deviations hurt.
- **Outputs / effects:** Determines effective range/endurance; off-cruise speeds reduce range below the cruise baseline.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Loiter's initial range-ring spike is **misleading** — it reflects the instantaneous low-speed reading before deceleration and sustained low-speed burn are accounted for. Both too-slow and too-fast deviate from optimum; the loss is qualitative (no fixed percentage given).
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** High

### Aircraft fuel economy near peak speed (a small speedup can improve economy)
- **Models:** For some aircraft, trimming a few knots off peak speed slightly lowers per-minute fuel burn even though it costs a little time — a small efficiency gain near the top of the speed band.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Ordered aircraft speed (knots); current per-minute fuel burn (kg/min); altitude (above 36,000 ft in example); external stores.
- **Behavior / rules:** On an **FA-18 above 36,000 ft**, ordering **470 kn vs 460 kn** changed per-minute burn from **30.96 kg/min to 30.48 kg/min** — a decrease. Framed as: going faster here (470) costs ~10 knots-per-hour [sic] but improves fuel economy by "basically **2 or 3%**." The improvement is **minuscule** (a tiny change in the blue range line). Useful to slightly extend range when already near peak.
- **Outputs / effects:** Per-minute fuel burn (kg/min) and thus total fuel required / range change by a small amount (~2–3% economy in the example).
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Effect is tiny (2–3%) and **platform/altitude-specific** — not a general rule. The relationship is **non-monotonic** vs the naive "faster = more fuel" assumption, which is why it's highlighted. Displayed burn includes external-stores penalty, so absolute kg/min is loadout-dependent.
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** Med

### Manual fuel-endurance calculation (the "hard way") with 5% reserve buffer
- **Models:** Hand-computing whether an aircraft has enough fuel to reach a destination using distance, speed, per-minute burn, and a fuel reserve margin.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Course distance in nautical miles (from F2 on waypoint, e.g. 3,100 nm); reserve fraction (**5%**); current speed in knots (e.g. 460 kn); current per-minute fuel burn (e.g. 30.96 kg/min above 36,000 ft); current fuel on board (e.g. ~12863 kg).
- **Behavior / rules:** Stated step-by-step: (1) Distance via F2 on the destination waypoint = **3,100 nm**. (2) Add 5% reserve: 3,100 × 0.05 = 155; 155 + 3,100 = **3,255 nm** planned distance. (3) Read burn rate from the unit's detailed readout: **30.96 kg/min** (the stores-modified number). (4) Flight time in hours = distance / speed = 3255 / 460 = **7.07 hours**. (5) To minutes: 7.07 × 60 = **424 minutes**. (6) Total fuel needed = minutes × burn = 424 × 30.96 = **13144 kg**. (7) Compare to onboard (**12863 kg**). The naive total (13144) slightly **EXCEEDS** onboard, but the trip is still feasible because the aircraft burns less as it gets lighter — and CMO's own readout showed onboard 12863 kg = **7 h 8 min** endurance, nearly matching the hand-calc's 7.07 hours, confirming the method.
- **Outputs / effects:** A go/no-go fuel feasibility estimate; total fuel required (kg) and flight time (min/hr) for the planned distance.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Naive linear calc **over-estimates** fuel needed because it ignores that the platform "burns less fuel as it gets lighter" — a result slightly over capacity can still be achievable. Must use the **stores-modified** burn number, not the baseline. CMO does not let you change the burn-rate display units (no tons/hour readout) — forcing manual conversion. Margins are tight ("really careful on those margins"). Jettisoning external stores reduces weight/burn (jettison button available).
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** High

### Weight-dependent fuel burn (lighter platform burns less)
- **Models:** As an aircraft consumes fuel and gets lighter (or jettisons stores), its fuel consumption rate drops, so real endurance exceeds a constant-rate estimate.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Current aircraft weight (fuel remaining + external stores); jettison action; per-minute burn rate.
- **Behavior / rules:** Stated explicitly: "**as you get lighter, you will burn less fuel**," which is why a constant-burn hand calculation (13144 kg needed) can exceed onboard fuel (12863 kg) yet the flight is still completable. CMO's internal endurance readout (12863 kg = 7h08m) already accounts for this decreasing burn, landing very close to the simplistic calc. **Jettisoning external stores** immediately reduces weight and consumption.
- **Outputs / effects:** Effective fuel burn rate decreases over the flight as weight drops; increases achievable range/endurance beyond a flat-rate prediction.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Makes naive flat-rate calculations conservative (pessimistic). External stores both add weight (raising burn) and can be jettisoned to cut it; the displayed per-minute burn is already "modified by external stores."
- **Source:** JVwUVxemDFk
- **Confidence:** High

## Cargo model & carrying capacity

### Cargo carrying-capacity model (mass / area / crew limits + per-host max-item size)
- **Models:** A unit's physical ability to carry cargo, constrained by weight, volume, and personnel space, plus a hard cap on the largest single item it can hold.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Host unit's cargo **Mass** capacity, cargo **Area** capacity, cargo **Crew/Pax** capacity; each candidate cargo item's mass, area, and crew/pax cost; per-host **maximum item size**.
- **Behavior / rules:** When editing cargo, items that cannot physically fit are shown in **RED** and cannot be carried. **Three independent budgets** must all be satisfied: cargo **Mass** (weight), cargo **Area** (volume/footprint), and cargo **Crew/Pax** (personnel space). Each item type has a fixed cost in these dimensions (example: one infantry section = **4 cargo-crew**). As items are added, Mass/Area/Crew totals are subtracted from capacity. Separately, each host has a limit on the **BIGGEST single thing** it can fit — on a small host (LPH) many items are red purely because they exceed the **max item size**, independent of remaining tonnage. Example: LPH troop capacity **1806**; 1806/4 = ~451 infantry sections by crew, demonstrated loading **1800 of 1806** (capacity nearly exhausted on crew with zero mass/area used because infantry add crew, not vehicle mass/area).
- **Outputs / effects:** Allowed/blocked (red) status per item; running totals of consumed Mass, Area, Crew/Pax vs capacity; the unit's reported "Cargo" count.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Infantry/personnel consume Crew/Pax but little/no Mass or Area; vehicles consume Mass+Area but no Pax. Red items may be blocked by max-item-size even when tonnage remains. A host can be full on one dimension (crew) while empty on others.

- **Confidence:** High

### Cargo modeled as mounts/weapons on a generic "landed detachment" facility

Per-item stats shown: e.g., a howitzer = **2 tons, 16 m² cargo area, crew of 6**. Ships display a top-level cargo capacity (size/mass/area).

### Load All (fill host to capacity) and per-host loading time
- **Models:** Bulk-loading shortcut that crams as many of a selected cargo type as will fit, with a real time cost to embark.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Selected cargo type/stack at the source; host's remaining capacity.
- **Behavior / rules:** Pressing "**Load All**" shoves as many of the selected items as possible into the host until a capacity limit is hit (e.g. a small boat tops out at **8 men**). Loading is **not instant** — it consumes simulated time proportional to amount: filling the boats took ~30 minutes; helicopters likewise ~30 min; aircraft load time "depends on how much you ask to get on board" (heavier asks take longer).
- **Outputs / effects:** Host filled to capacity; embark timer running before the host is mission-ready.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Must confirm with **OK** or the load silently doesn't apply (presenter repeatedly checks because the add can fail to register).

### Cargo load/unload timing is per item type (not instantaneous)
- **Models:** Loading and unloading cargo consumes real in-game time that varies by cargo type and platform.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Cargo item type (troops vs. mortar/heavy-weapon teams vs. munition containers); platform; current time-to-ready countdown (F6 status); amount/weight loaded.
- **Behavior / rules:** Each holder shows a **time-to-ready countdown** while loading/unloading; there is no progress bar, only a countdown number. Loading troops onto helicopters took on the order of **~50 seconds**; **mortar teams ~15 minutes** — different cargo classes have very different load times. Container examples: **ISO container off a truck = 45 minutes**; loading a **20ft container onto a CH-53 = ~30 minutes**; ships loading **40-footers ~1 hour** (another ship ~50 min — "load the ship molecule by molecule"). ### Port/pier cargo storage = whole port acts as one unit, but cargo MUST be integrated into the PIER (not the port's buildings)
- **Models:** How a seaport exposes cargo for loading onto ships — fundamentally different from an airport.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Port composite (pier + fuel + ammo bunker + warehouse, etc.); the **PIER** object specifically (selected via numpad 9); ships docked at the port; separate barracks/building stocks.
- **Behavior / rules:** Ports are **NOT grouped the way airports are**. Historically you'd build an adjacent barracks, load it, and deliver via **MISSION** (not manually). Pier size is adjustable. A "**single unit Port**" database object bundles piers + components ready-made and lets you do everything in one operation. A combined **LST-type** vessel can carry aircraft + boats + cargo simultaneously and handles it well.
- **Outputs / effects:** Cargo on the pier becomes loadable by docked ships; mis-placed cargo (in a building) is invisible to ships.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Key gotcha: airports treat the whole airport as the cargo unit; ports require cargo on the **pier** specifically. Single-unit-port simplifies this. Manual port loading ignores cargo in separate buildings.

- **Confidence:** High

## Cargo missions (automated ferry, delivery, transfer)

### Cargo Mission types — TRANSFER vs DELIVERY (base-to-base vs drop-on-ground)
- **Models:** Two cargo-mission sub-modes that determine the destination semantics of moved cargo.

A Transfer mission opens a transfer panel where you pick which source(s) feed the carrier and what to send. Picking the **WRONG sub-mode** is a common mistake (choosing Transfer when you meant Delivery results in cargo not landing where expected). Cargo missions **cannot be created via the quick-mission path** — the mission type MUST be set to "Cargo."

- **Outputs / effects:** Spawned paratroop ground units; aircraft RTB after manual drop.

- **Source:** t-dHCPdXbW0
- **Confidence:** Med

### Naval cargo missions (ship operations / beach landing) and shuttle behavior
- **Models:** Mothership-launched landing craft shuttle cargo to a beach and can detach to attack ground targets.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Mission area spanning water+land; landing-zone point; mothership; sub-ship type (LCVP/LCU/LCAC); cargo type; cruise speed; loading time.
- **Behavior / rules:** Define an area covering both water and land with a **land landing-zone point**. Assign sub-ships (e.g., LCVPs) and pick mothership + cargo type + cruise speed. Sub-ships can be ordered to **LEAVE the mothership carrying cargo and attack ground targets** en route. Behavior: the mothership/port loads infantry onto landing craft (**~30 min per loading operation**) then they run to the beach; after dropping cargo they automatically **RTB, reload, and return** to repeat (semi-automatic shuttle) until all troops are delivered. **SAME descent/unload bug as para-drop:** craft ride up onshore and loop without unloading, so you must **manually right-click > unload cargo** on each. A single big **LST/alligator dumps everyone at once** (resulting unit reached battalion strength), vs. piecemeal landing craft reaching ~company strength.
- **Outputs / effects:** Spawned ground units onshore; shuttling landing craft; aircraft/helo from the mothership can also assist.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Big ships need to reach the zone and often **won't auto-unload** — manually stop and unload; ~30 min infantry load time; manual unload required due to the same loop bug.
- **Source:** t-dHCPdXbW0
- **Confidence:** Med

## Underway Replenishment (UNREP) — resupply at sea

### Underway Replenishment (UNREP) — alongside refuel & rearm at sea
- **Models:** Ships replenish fuel and munitions from a provider vessel by steaming alongside it, transferring continuously over time while underway.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Receiver ship's connection points (refuel from port/starboard, replenish from port/starboard, each often x2 redundant); fuel types carried (e.g. MGO gas fuel + a secondary aviation-fuel tank); provider vessel type and its onboard stocks; weather state; per-side selection (numpad 9); commands: select provider (auto/manual), rearm (auto/manual), and per-item rearm picks.
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) A ship advertises refuel/replenish stations per side; "**x2**" means a **redundant backup** station (if one breaks use the other) — it does NOT mean two simultaneous providers per side. (2) Right-click → unwrap → "**select provider automatically**" makes the receiver **break formation** (without truly leaving the group) and steer to the nearest capable/available provider ("heading to refuel point"). (3) Choosing "**rearm automatically**" (and picking which items, if available) can **change the chosen provider mid-route** — it re-selects a provider that has everything needed (e.g. switches from the carrier to the dedicated supply ship). (4) Refuel only transfers fuel types the provider **actually carries** (a carrier provides AVIATION fuel only, not MGO, since it carries none). (5) **Multiple receivers can draw from ONE provider simultaneously** (carrier + Arleigh Burke both rearming from the same supply ship, draining it). (6) Rates are **very slow** — fuel quantities are in the millions of kg and tick down ~100 kg per glance even at 15x; item replenishment is similarly slow. (7) **Manual** provider selection lets you designate the exact provider/receiver pairing.
- **Outputs / effects:** Receiver fuel and magazine items increase over time; provider stocks deplete; receiver temporarily peels off then rejoins.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** "x2" stations are **redundancy, NOT parallel multi-provider capacity** per side; carriers historically refuel their escorts (provider need not be a dedicated oiler); auto-rearm can silently re-route to a better-stocked provider; transfers are extremely time-consuming; a carrier-group group-order makes the receiver do useless "power donuts" around the carrier — select by unit (numpad 9) instead.
- **Source:** Xz9A8IsvFpI
- **Confidence:** High

### UNREP simultaneous-customer limits and weather gate
- **Models:** A provider can service a capped number of receivers at once, with different caps for fueling vs. replenishment, and cannot conduct UNREP at all in bad weather.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Provider capacity: **refuel = up to 4 ships at a time; replenishment = up to 6 ships at a time**; weather state.
- **Behavior / rules:** Stated limits: the AOE provider can **REFUEL up to 4 ships simultaneously** and **REPLENISH (dry/munitions) up to 6 ships simultaneously** ("...one, two, three, four ships... refuel... and replenishment he can do six ships at a time"). In **BAD WEATHER you cannot conduct UNREP at all** ("keep in mind in bad weather you can't do this"). UNREP is slow and leaves the cluster of ships **highly vulnerable** (a single missile could "end this entire fleet" while they're tightly grouped).
- **Outputs / effects:** Limits how many receivers a provider serves concurrently; weather can fully disable UNREP.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Different concurrency caps for fuel (4) vs. replenishment (6); narrator flags 4/6 as unrealistically high; bad weather disables UNREP entirely; tightly-grouped UNREP ships are a major vulnerability.
- **Source:** Xz9A8IsvFpI
- **Confidence:** Med

### Replenishment-ship classes and their cargo specializations
- **Models:** Different US Navy replenishment-ship classes carry different commodities (fuel vs. dry stores vs. munitions vs. all-in-one), determining what they can provide.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Ship class: **T-AOE-6 Fast Combat Support** (fuel + dry stores, fast); **AOR Wichita-type** (older all-in-one: fuel + dry stores, many cranes/davits); **Henry J. Kaiser-class oiler** (petroleum/oil/lubricants, ~20–22 knots, fast); **Lewis & Clark-class T-AKE** dry cargo (munitions/dry, smaller, side openings for missile transfer).
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) The **fast combat support ship (AOE)** does a bit of everything and is fast. (2) The legacy **AOR** was an all-in-one (fuel + dry stores). (3) **Kaiser-class oilers** carry the things that burn (fuel/POL) and are fast (~20–22 kt). (4) **Lewis & Clark T-AKE** is **dry-cargo only**, engineered for dropping off missiles/dry stores. (5) A receiver **auto-routes to whichever provider actually has the needed commodity** — so class dictates suitability. (6) A dry-cargo ship will **NOT have munitions by default** in the editor; the designer must stock its magazine (see next rule).
- **Outputs / effects:** Determines which fuel/munition commodities a provider can deliver; receivers route to the matching class.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** A class lacking a commodity simply can't provide it (carrier has no MGO; T-AKE has no fuel); dry-cargo magazines start empty; ship sizes are noted as possibly oversized in-game.
- **Source:** Xz9A8IsvFpI
- **Confidence:** Med

### Stocking a replenishment ship's magazine (designer pre-load of UNREP munitions)
- **Models:** A supply/dry-cargo ship's transferable munitions are defined by adding entries to its magazine, which it then holds for replenishment (but cannot fire).
- **Inputs / parameters:** Ship's Magazines page with a special "add munitions" area; munition type (e.g. RGM/UGM Tomahawk, carbon-filament warheads); requested quantity (e.g. 100).
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) Click the dry-cargo ship → **Magazines** → use the dedicated **add-munitions** area to add desired rounds (typed by name) and set any quantity (e.g. **100 Tomahawks**). (2) These munitions are now available to be **replenished into other ships** via UNREP. (3) The ship can carry/hold them but **CANNOT fire them** off its own deck. (4) Stocked items become the **source pool** that receivers draw from during rearm.
- **Outputs / effects:** Provider gains a magazine of transferable munitions usable for UNREP rearm of other ships.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Provider can't fire its stocked munitions (hold-only); without designer stocking, the ship has none to give; arbitrarily large quantities can be added in editor.
- **Source:** Xz9A8IsvFpI
- **Confidence:** Med

### Provider-as-group-lead trick to UNREP while keeping course/formation
- **Models:** Making the supply ship the group leader lets the whole formation keep its destination/course while escorts come alongside it to replenish on the move.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Group membership (add supply ship into the group); **F4 → set as group chief/leader**; a formation layout; per-receiver manual provider selection + rearm orders.
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) Group the supply ship with the combatants and **set it as group leader** (F4 → set as leader). (2) Place ships in a **formation** to simplify the geometry. (3) Then order each receiver (right-click → unwrap → select provider manually → choose the lead supply ship → rearm) to draw from the lead. (4) Receivers slide up alongside the (faster) provider and replenish **while the group continues** toward its destination — "moving and resupplying simultaneously." (5) Receivers will ask the provider to **slow** ("hit the brakes") because closing speed matters for coming alongside. (6) A single provider can service everyone behind it as long as it has stock.
- **Outputs / effects:** Whole group keeps moving toward objective while escorts refuel/rearm from the lead provider continuously.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Counterintuitive to make the supply ship lead, but it works; speed mismatch — fast provider forces receivers to manage closing speed; building a mission for the same effect just replicates the per-unit behavior, so grouping is simpler.
- **Source:** Xz9A8IsvFpI
- **Confidence:** Med

### Air delivery of a munition container to a ship cannot rearm it (current limitation)
- **Models:** Although an aircraft can land on a ship and drop an ISO container of munitions on deck, there is currently no way to unpack that container into the ship's launchers at sea.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Carrier-capable cargo aircraft (e.g. C-2 Greyhound) carrying an ISO 20ft container holding a Tomahawk; receiving ship.
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) A **C-2 Greyhound** can carry an ISO 20ft container (with a Tomahawk inside) and land/unload it onto a ship's deck. (2) But you **CANNOT open that container at sea** to extract the missile, and even if you could, getting it into the destroyer's launchers across the ocean isn't supported. (3) So container-by-air is **not a viable at-sea rearm path** — UNREP alongside is the working method. (4) **Exception:** an aircraft that is itself loaded out (ordnance load) **CAN drop its own load off at the carrier** (transferring an aircraft's own weapons to a carrier works, unlike unpacking a cargo container into a ship).
- **Outputs / effects:** Container sits inert on deck; no rearm of the ship's launchers occurs from it.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Explicit current-version limitation ("at this time"); distinguishes a loaded aircraft offloading its own ordnance to a carrier (works) vs. unpacking a cargo container into a ship (does not work).
- **Source:** Xz9A8IsvFpI
- **Confidence:** Med

## Guided weapons (PGMs) — delivery constraints that gate stored ordnance

### PGM guidance families and their engagement constraints
- **Models:** Each guidance type imposes different line-of-sight, range, weather, and post-launch-freedom rules.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Guidance type (command/radio, electro-optical, EO+datalink, laser, GPS/INS, hybrid laser+GPS, active radar); weather/cloud layer; laser-pod altitude limit & range; line-of-sight; target motion (fixed vs moving).
- **Behavior / rules:** **Command/radio-guided** (e.g., RB-05, Walleye): very precise but the **FIRING unit must follow the weapon in** (vulnerable, limited range, only ONE guided at a time). **Electro-optical** (Maverick/Walleye): fire-and-forget but you must **SEE the target at launch**; limited range; some early EO were TV-guided and unusable at night. **EO+datalink** (GBU-62): launch with line-of-sight, then fly away while still adjusting it down — great vs moving ships. **Laser-guided** (GBU-24 etc.): must maintain line-of-sight and shine a laser; can track **MOVING** targets; laser pods have range/altitude limits — early pods (e.g., LANTIRN) won't illuminate **above 10,000 ft**, and a cloud layer forces the aircraft below the clouds (exposing it) or the weapon can't guide; works at night. **GPS/INS** (JDAM): takes **COORDINATES** not a target; ignores clouds (drops through and self-guides) but **CANNOT hit moving targets**. Inertial-only bombs exist (e.g., CBU-107) but rarely carried. **Hybrid laser+GPS** (GBU-54): uses laser when available, else falls back to GPS coordinates. **Active-radar** (AGM-114L): own radar seeker, can be fired then switch off; jammable.
- **Outputs / effects:** Hit/miss governed by LOS, laser/altitude limits, weather, and target motion.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** GPS bombs can't hit movers unless they have their own seeker; laser needs LOS+laser+altitude compliance; PGMs (unlike dumb bombs) can be **intercepted by missile-defense**; glide weapons (JSOW ~50 mi, SDB) are slow and vulnerable to AAA/SAM if not suppressed.
- **Source:** 0ZiTDxp6kQ4
- **Confidence:** High

### PGM weapon-release defaults and salvo/fire-rate limits
- **Models:** Default WRA tends to over-expend scarce guided weapons; launchers fire only a few PGMs per salvo.
- **Inputs / parameters:** WRA default impacts-per-target (often **2**); rounds-per-target; number of PGMs carried (often 1–2); per-platform salvo rate; release altitude.
- **Behavior / rules:** Default WRA for most guided weapons is **TWO per target** — must be overridden because aircraft "rarely carry more than one or two" guided weapons, so the default wastes them dropping multiple on a target needing one. Set impacts to **one round/single-unit** to conserve and double coverage (e.g., GBU-10 at **643** dmg vs ~600 HP hangar → one per hangar). Platforms **fire only so many PGMs at a time:** a **B-52H** with 24 GBU-JDAMs released only **~2 at a time** and descended to **10,000 ft**, failing to dump its whole load; **Tomahawks** completed the same job in one salvo. General tip: match target HP to weapon damage and don't drop two 500-damage bombs on a 100-HP target.
- **Outputs / effects:** Conserved/over-expended PGMs depending on WRA; partial salvos from slow-releasing platforms.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** Some aircraft leave with ordnance still attached after a single attack (wasted attack) — check eMCON/loadout setting; AI re-attacks the same target with sections, so disable force-flight-size or split attacks manually.
- **Source:** 0ZiTDxp6kQ4
- **Confidence:** Med

### Long-range ballistic surface-to-surface munition flight & impact (ATACMS/SSM)
- **Models:** Surface-launched ballistic rounds (e.g. MGM-168 ATACMS, generic SSM) fly a high ballistic arc and impact with warhead-type-dependent damage.
- **Inputs / parameters:** Munition type/warhead (unitary HE vs. submunition/cluster), launch range vs. weapon max range, apogee altitude.
- **Behavior / rules:** (1) Round boosts to very high altitude (observed **~123–127 km** apogee) then arcs back down — explicitly **ballistic**, not an air-breathing/HE cruise weapon. (2) **Unitary-HE** warhead "does not malfunction" and produces a single concentrated detonation (good vs. hard point targets like a building). (3) **Submunition/cluster** variants spread/sprinkle shrapnel over an area ("sparkles") — less effective vs. a single hardened building but devastating vs. exposed/clustered infantry. (4) Extremely long ranges mean you can easily be **out of range and not realize it**; respect the weapon's range envelope. (5) During terminal descent the round is theoretically engageable by high-end SAMs (SA-10/SA-21 named) but is a very fast, difficult intercept.
- **Outputs / effects:** Target structure/units destroyed per warhead type; area vs. point damage pattern differs by warhead.
- **Edge cases / quirks:** "Out of range" is easy to miss given the long reach; unitary = point kill, cluster = area spray; terminal phase is the only realistic SAM-engagement window and it is hard.
- **Source:** 1A7ltgegnQI
- **Confidence:** Low
